About Kolam

Discovering the Sacred Art of Indian Heritage

What is Kolam?

Kolam is a traditional art form practiced primarily in South India, where intricate geometric patterns are drawn on the ground using rice flour or chalk powder. These sacred designs are created daily at dawn by women in front of their homes, temples, and other sacred spaces.

The word "Kolam" derives from the Tamil words "kol" (beauty) and "am" (creation), literally meaning "beautiful creation." This ancient practice dates back over 5,000 years and represents one of the world's oldest forms of geometric art and mathematical expression.

Did you know? Kolam patterns are created using a grid of dots, and the challenge lies in connecting these dots with continuous lines without lifting the hand, creating complex geometric forms.

Types of Kolam

Pulli Kolam

The most common type, drawn by connecting dots (pulli) with curved lines to form intricate patterns. The challenge is to create continuous loops without lifting the hand.

Sikku Kolam

Free-hand designs drawn without dots, allowing for more creative expression. These often incorporate nature motifs like flowers, leaves, and animals.

Neli Kolam

Shadow kolams where the background is filled with rice flour, creating a negative space effect. These are often used during festivals and special occasions.

Colored Kolam

Modern variations using colored powders, particularly popular during festivals like Pongal and Diwali, adding vibrancy to traditional designs.

How to Create Kolam

1

Prepare the Surface

Clean the ground thoroughly and sprinkle water to create a smooth, even surface. Traditionally done at dawn on the doorstep.

2

Place the Dots

Mark dots (pulli) in a grid pattern using your fingers. Start with simple grids like 3x3 or 5x5 for beginners.

3

Connect with Lines

Using rice flour held between thumb and forefinger, draw curved lines connecting the dots to form patterns.

4

Complete the Design

Ensure all lines are connected in continuous loops. No dot should be left unconnected, and no lines should intersect improperly.
